XVII. Note respecting the demonstration of the binomial theorem inserted in the last volume of the Philosophical Transactions. By Thomas Knight, Esq. Communicated by Taylor Combe, Esq. Sec. R. S. Read April 17, 1817. In looking into Mr. Spence's ingenious "Essay on Logarithmic Transcendents," a work published in 1809, but which I have been so unfortunate as never to have seen till within the last fortnight, I was not a little surprised to find that a demonstration of the binomial theorem, similar to the one I had the honour to present to the Royal Society, had been already given by that writer. The same may be said of the first proposition of the preceding Paper on the construction of Logarithms. Having made this acknowledgment, I shall perhaps be pardoned for observing, that Mr. Spence is not particularly happy in the manner of developing the kind of functions he treats of in his preface. I shall endeavour to give the solution of a class of equations of which he (Pref. p. vii.) has considered a particular case: with this we will begin. It is proposed to develope the function which has this property, viz. \[ \varphi(1+x) + \varphi(1+y) + \varphi(1+z) = \varphi(1+x.1+y) + \varphi(1+x.1+z) + \varphi(1+y.1+z) - \varphi(1+x.1+y.1+z). \] Assume \( \varphi(1+x) = A + A'x + A''x^2 + A'''x^3 + \ldots + A''''x^n + \), and after making the requisite substitutions in the given equation, we see immediately that $A = 0$, and that $A'$ and $A''$ are arbitrary. Then, to find the law of the coefficients, in this and other similar cases, where there are any number of independent quantities, $x, x', x'', \ldots, x'^{n}$, transpose all the equation to one side, and find the coefficient of the first power of $x'^{n-1}$ in the former coefficient, then again, in this last, the coefficient of the first power of $x'^{n-2}$: and having arrived in this manner at the coefficient of $x'$, it will have the form $a + bx + cx^2 + \ldots + rx^n$, and the equation $r = 0$ will give the law sought for. In the present case, putting $\rho = x + (1 + x)z$, $\sigma = 1 + x + (1 + x)z$, we find, by equalling to $0$ the coefficient of the first power of $y$, $$0 = A' + 2A''(x + z) + 3A'''(x^2 + z^2) + \ldots + (n + 1)A''^{(n+1)}(x^n + z^n) + \ldots + nA'^{n}$$ $$- A'\sigma - 2A''\sigma\rho - 3A'''\sigma\rho^2 - \ldots - (n + 1)(A''^{(n+1)}\sigma\rho^n)$$ If in this we equal to $0$ the coefficient of the first power of $z$, there arises $0 = 2A'' - A'x - 2A''(1 + 3x + 2x^2)$ $$- 3A'''(2x + 5x^2 + 3x^3)$$ $$- 4A''''(3x^2 + 7x^3 + 4x^4)$$ $$\ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots$$ $$- nA'^{n}(n - 1 \cdot x^{n-2} + 2n - 1 \cdot x^{n-1} + nx^n)$$ $$\ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots$$ whence we find for the general law of the coefficients ($n > 2$), $$n(n - 1)A''^{n} + 2n - 3 \cdot n - 1 \cdot A''^{(n-1)} + (n - 2)^2A''^{(n-2)} = 0 \ldots \ldots (1)$$ From which let us suppose that we have calculated a few of the coefficients, and arrived at the result of Mr. Spence, viz. of the binomial theorem. \[ \phi(1+x) = A'(x - \frac{x^3}{2\cdot3} + \frac{5x^4}{2\cdot3\cdot4} - \ldots) + A''L^2(1+x) \ldots (2) \] nothing can be easier than to find the value of the remaining series; for it is quite obvious, from the equation expressing the property of the function, that \( L(1+x) \) is a particular value of \( \phi(1+x) \). The same also appears from equation (1), in which, if we put \( A''...(n-2) = \frac{1}{n-2}, A''...(n-1) = \frac{-1}{n-1}, A'...n = \frac{1}{n} \), the left hand member vanishes. Make then, in equation (2), \( A' = 1, A'' = \frac{-1}{2} \), and it becomes \[ L(1+x) = x - \frac{x^3}{2\cdot3} + \frac{5x^4}{2\cdot3\cdot4} - \&c. - \frac{1}{2}L^2(1+x), \text{ whence} \] \[ x - \frac{x^3}{2\cdot3} + \frac{5x^4}{2\cdot3\cdot4} - \&c. = L(1+x) + \frac{1}{2}L^2(1+x), \text{ and finally} \] \[ \phi(1+x) = A'\left\{ L(1+x) + \frac{1}{2}L^2(1+x) \right\} + A''L^2(1+x) \] Let us now endeavour to develope the function next in order of the same class, viz. \( \phi(1+x) \), having the following property, \[ \phi(1+w) + \phi(1+x) + \phi(1+y) + \phi(1+z) = \phi(1+x\cdot1+y) \] \[ + \phi(1+x\cdot1+z) + \phi(1+y\cdot1+z) + \phi(1+w\cdot1+x) + \phi(1+w\cdot1+y) + \phi(1+w\cdot1+z) - \ldots \ldots (3) \] \[ \phi(1+x\cdot1+y\cdot1+z) = \phi(1+w\cdot1+y\cdot1+z) - \phi(1+w\cdot1+x\cdot1+y\cdot1+z) \] Assume \( \phi(1+x) = A + A'x + A''x^2 + \ldots \ldots + A''...n x^n + \) and make the requisite substitutions; we shall find \( A = 0, A', A'' \) arbitrary. Then to have the law of the coefficients, find, according to the rule, the coefficient of \( x \) in the coefficient of \( z \) in the coefficient of \( y \), and, comparing in this the coefficients of the powers of \( w \), we find MDCCCXVII. To find the upper series we have the same means as in the last Problem, \( L(1+x) \) being a particular value of \( \varphi(1+x) \); make then \( A' = 1, A'' = -\frac{1}{2}, A''' = \frac{1}{3} \); our equation becomes \( L(1+x) = x - \frac{1}{24} x^4 + \frac{9}{120} x^5 - \) \[- \frac{1}{2} \left\{ L^2(1+x) + L^3(1+x) \right\} \\ + \frac{1}{3} L^2(1+x) \] whence \( x - \frac{1}{24} x^4 + \frac{9}{120} x^5 - \ldots \text{&c.} = L(1+x) + \frac{1}{2} L^2(1+x) \\ + \frac{1}{6} L^3(1+x) \), and \[ \varphi(1+x) = A' \left\{ L(1+x) + \frac{1}{2} L^2(1+x) + \frac{1}{6} L^3(1+x) \right\} \\ + A'' \left\{ L^2(1+x) + L^3(1+x) \right\} \\ + A''' L^3(1+x) \] which is the complete solution of the proposed equation. As, however, this result has been obtained by the inspection of only a few terms of two series, a doubt may be entertained with respect to its truth: make therefore \( w = x = y = z \), in equation (3), it will become \( 4\varphi(1+x) = 6\varphi(1+x)^2 \\ - 4\varphi(1+x)^3 + \varphi(1+x)^4 \), which by the substitution of the value found above for $\phi(1+x)$ is found to be identical, and the truth of the solution is proved. We may now attempt the solution of the general problem, viz. Let $x, x', x'', x''', \ldots, x'''\cdots$ be independent quantities; it is required to find $\phi(1+x)$ from the following equation, $$\sum \phi(1+x'''\cdots) = \sum \phi(1+x'''\cdots \cdot 1+x'''\cdots n) - \sum \phi(1+x'''\cdots \cdot 1+x'''\cdots r) +$$ $$\sum \phi(1+x'''\cdots \cdot 1+x'''\cdots n \cdot 1+x'''\cdots r \cdot 1+x'''\cdots s) - \sum \phi(1+x'''\cdots \cdot 1+x'''\cdots n \cdot 1+x'''\cdots r \cdot 1+x'''\cdots s) + \&c.$$ Assume as usual $\phi(1+x) = A + A'x + A''x^2 + A'''x^3 + \&c.$; but instead of attempting to find the law of the coefficients, we may easily convince ourselves that $\phi(1+x)$ will have the following form, viz. $$\phi(1+x) = A' \left\{ L(1+x) + \alpha''L^2(1+x) + \alpha'''L^3(1+x) + \right.$$ $$\alpha''''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \alpha'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \right\}$$ $$+ A'' \left\{ L^2(1+x) + \beta''''L^3(1+x) + \beta'''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \beta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \right\}$$ $$+ A''' \left\{ L^3(1+x) + \gamma''''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \gamma'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \right\}$$ $$+ A'''\cdots p L^p(1+x)$$ This form evidently includes, as particular solutions, $L(1+x), L^2(1+x), L^3(1+x), \ldots, L^p(1+x):$ and, by means of these particular solutions, we are enabled to find the coefficients $\alpha'', \alpha''', \alpha''''$, &c., $\beta'', \beta''', \beta''''$, &c., $\gamma'', \gamma''', \gamma''''$, &c. For let $$L(1+x) = x + b''x^2 + b'''x^3 + b''''x^4 +$$ $$L^2(1+x) = x^2 + c''x^3 + c'''x^4 +$$ $$L^3(1+x) = x^3 + d''x^4 +$$ $$\&c. \quad \&c.$$ By changing $A'$, $A''$, $A'''$, &c. into the coefficients of each of these expressions, successively, we have $p$ particular values of equation (4); viz. $$L(1+x) = L(1+x) + \alpha''L^2(1+x) + \alpha'''L^3(1+x) + \alpha''''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \alpha'''\cdots p L^p(1+x)$$ $$+ b'' \{ L^2(1+x) + \beta'''L^3(1+x) + \beta''''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \beta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ b''' \{ L^3(1+x) + \gamma'''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \gamma'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ b'''' \{ L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \delta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ b'''\cdots p \times L^p(1+x)$$ from which we derive the equations $b'' + \alpha'' = 0$, $b''' + b''\beta''' + \alpha''' = 0$, $b'''' + b'''y''' + b''\beta'''' + \alpha'''' = 0$, &c.; next we have $$L^2(1+x) = L^2(1+x) + \beta'''L^3(1+x) + \beta''''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \beta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x)$$ $$= c''' \{ L^3(1+x) + \gamma'''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \gamma'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ c'''' \{ L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \delta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ c'''\cdots p \times L^p(1+x)$$ whence we get the equations $c''' + \beta''' = 0$, $c'''' + c'''y''' + \beta'''' = 0$, &c. The next particular solution is $$L^3(1+x) = L^3(1+x) + \gamma'''L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \gamma'''\cdots p L^p(1+x)$$ $$+ d'''' \{ L^4(1+x) + \ldots + \delta'''\cdots p L^p(1+x) \}$$ $$+ d'''\cdots p \times L^p(1+x)$$ whence $d'''' + \gamma'''' = 0$, &c. and by proceeding in the same way we have as many equations as the coefficients which are to be determined. So much for the expansion of these functions. What Mr. Spence means by the note in page ix. of his Preface, where he speaks of the integral $\int \frac{dx}{x} = \phi(x)$ an equation which is evidently impossible, I am unable to form the smallest conjecture. Papcastle, March 3, 1817.
