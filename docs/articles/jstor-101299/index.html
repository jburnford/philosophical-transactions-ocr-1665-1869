But the Principal uses in Physick, for which he commends the volatile salt and spirit made of the horns and blood of Stags, is, its piercing, opening, attenuating, absterive, discissing vertue. He intersperseth here and there, as he hath occasion, many Philosophical and Spagirical remarques: e.g. How all Acids change the nature of volatile Salts: How nature produceth the volatile Salt out of Acids or fixed Salts: How plants, and divers parts of Animals may by their volatile Salts be Chymically represented: by what method the volatile Salt of Harts-horn may be freed from its ungrateful smell, without much impairing its virtue: why Harts-horns taken off from the beast between the middle of August, and that of September, yield more and stronger spirits: whether Harts-horn be better and more efficacious, when calcined, or crude, or prepared by a steamy heat? &c. And among the many Medical prescripts, set down here, the Author gives us the Podagrick unguent of the so much famed Franciscus Jos. Borbhi, made up of almost all the parts of a stag: which how far it deserves commendations, must be learn'd from experience. V.
