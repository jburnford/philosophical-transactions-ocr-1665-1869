# Tanning Materials, Astringents & Oak Bark Alternatives in the Philosophical Transactions (1665-1869)

A chronological analysis of how substances like terra japonica, catechu, oak bark, and other astringents evolved from medical curiosities to industrial tanning materials in the scientific literature of the Royal Society.

## Phase 1: Early Trade Goods & Curiosities (1665-1700)

### 1673 - VALONIA: Earliest Alternative Tanning Material Mentioned!

**[An Accompt of Two Books](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/articles/jstor-101345/)**
- [Download MD](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-101345.md) | [Download JSON](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-101345.json)
- **Context:** Description of Turkish leather-making practices
- **Key passage:** "As to the Turky way of dressing Leather... In cleaning of their leather, they use Lime and Album Graecum; and **instead of Barks of trees, they employ Valonia, a sort of acorn growing on the Oakes**."
- Author suggests English acorns might work better: "I am persuaded, that our Acorns in England, if they could be spar'd for it, would perform the like effect, and perhaps better; seeing that many times the **Valonia burns the Leather** so much as to make it little serviceable"
- **Significance:** Earliest mention of a non-bark tanning alternative in Phil. Trans.!

### 1799 - George Biggin: First Systematic Bark Comparison

**[Experiments to Determine the Quantity of Tanning Principle and Gallic Acid Contained in the Bark of Various Trees](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/articles/jstor-107036/)**
- [Download MD](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-107036.md) | [Download JSON](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-107036.json)
- **Context:** Industrial chemistry - seeking alternatives
- Communicated by **Joseph Banks**

- Tests multiple tree barks for tannin content

### 1803 - HUMPHRY DAVY: The Landmark Paper on Tanning Chemistry

**[An Account of Some Experiments and Observations on the Constituent Parts of Certain Astringent Vegetables; And on Their Operation in Tanning](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/articles/jstor-107074/)**
- [Download MD](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-107074.md) | [Download JSON](https://jburnford.github.io/philosophical-transactions-ocr-1665-1869/downloads/jstor-107074.json)
- **Context:** Comprehensive industrial chemistry - BREAKTHROUGH
- **42 pages** - the definitive early paper on tanning science
- Communicated by **Joseph Banks**
- Conducted at the **Royal Institution** starting September 1801

**Key findings on CATECHU (Terra Japonica):**
> "The discovery of the tanning powers of catechu is owing to the President of the Royal Society [Joseph Banks], who, concluding from its sensible properties that it contained tannin, furnished me, in December, 1801, with a quantity for chemical examination."

- Catechu tans skin in **less than a month**
- Leather is "a much deeper colour than that tanned with galls"
- Bombay and Bengal catechu perform equally well
- Contains about 34 grains tannin per 41 grains solid matter
- Catechu comes from "a species of the **Mimosa**" (Acacia catechu) - first scientific identification of source
- Also tests: oak bark, Leicester willow bark, Spanish chestnut bark
- Willow bark nearly equal to oak bark in tanning power!

**Key findings on MYROBALANS:**
- Davy tests "**Myrobalans from the East Indies**" (fruit of Terminalia species)
- Notes their infusion "effervesced with the carbonated alkalis" - different from other astringents
- Gives a "dense precipitate" that "was almost immediately redisolved"
- "Strongly reddened litmus-paper, and gave a bright black with the solutions of iron"
- **First scientific analysis of myrobalans as tanning material**

**Other materials tested:**
- Sicilian sumach: 165 grains soluble matter, 78 grains tannin per ounce
- Malaga sumach: 156 grains soluble matter, 79 grains tannin per ounce

**Section III specifically titled:** "EXPERIMENTS AND OBSERVATIONS ON CATECHU OR TERRA JAPONICA"

## Key Themes & Analysis

### 1. Oak Bark Scarcity Was an Old Problem
Charles Howard in **1674** already mentions "where Oak is scarce" - this concern predates the Napoleonic Wars by over a century.

### 2. Joseph Banks' Systematic Campaign (1789-1803)
Banks appears to have orchestrated a systematic search for tanning alternatives:
- 1789: Commissions botanical surveys from India mentioning terra japonica sources
- 1799: Sponsors Biggin's bark comparison experiments (via Duke of Bedford)
- 1801 (December): Identifies catechu's tanning potential, supplies it to Davy
- 1803: Sponsors Davy's landmark paper proving catechu works

### 3. The Context Evolution of Terra Japonica/Catechu

| Period | Primary Context | Key Property Noted |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1693-1750s | Materia medica (medicine) | Astringent for cough, diarrhea |
| 1749 | Antiseptic research | Resists putrefaction |
| 1789 | Botanical survey | Powerful astringent |
| 1801 | Banks' hypothesis | Contains tannin |
| 1803 | Industrial chemistry | Tans leather rapidly |

### 4. Substances Found vs. NOT Found in Phil. Trans.

**FOUND (with tanning context):**
- **Valonia** (acorn cups) - 1673, described as Turkish tanning alternative to bark
- **Myrobalans** (Terminalia fruits) - 1700 as medicine, 1803 tested for tanning by Davy
- **Catechu/Terra Japonica** (Acacia/Mimosa extract) - 1693 medicine â†’ 1803 tanning
- **Sumach** (Sicilian & Malaga) - 1774 dyeing, 1803 tanning analysis

**NOT FOUND (may appear in trade/industrial publications):**
